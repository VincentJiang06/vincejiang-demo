Cobra Effect

Pay a bounty for cobras, and people start breeding cobras.

Requires B01 Goodhart's Law Unlocks B06 Rewarding A, Hoping for B B12 Principal-Agent K06 Planned Economy: Death by Quota

The story goes that in colonial India the government wanted to wipe out cobras, so it paid a bounty for every dead snake handed in. Citizens quickly worked something out: the corpses did not have to come from the wild, and breeding snakes yourself was more reliable. When the government discovered the bounty was having no effect and cancelled it, the breeders' snakes were worthless, so they let them go. The result: more snakes than at the start. The German economist Horst Siebert used it as a book title in 2001, and the "cobra effect" has been shorthand for perverse incentives ever since.

Put the honesty boundary up front: this story has no reliable historical source. Even the place name "Delhi" was added by later hands, and Siebert's original book only tells a general bounty legend from British India. The mechanism holds, the story is doubtful. The version with an archive behind it happened in Hanoi, and comes below.

The archived version has more absurd detail, and is more credible. In 1902 the government of French Indochina, guarding against plague, went after the rats in Hanoi's sewers. At first it paid per whole rat, then found that troublesome and switched to paying a bounty on rat tails, one cent each: to cut off a tail you first need a dead rat, which sounds unobjectionable.

Before long, sanitation officials started seeing a strange animal around the city: large numbers of living rats with no tails. The trappers had worked out exactly what the snake breeders in the legend worked out. Tails convert to money, and the rat itself is the capital that produces tails. Cut the tail, release the rat, let it keep breeding to produce the next generation of tails. Dedicated rat farms even appeared in the suburbs. The scheme was abandoned. The historian Michael Vann found the case files marked "destruction of harmful animals: rats" in the French overseas archives in 1995. Every detail above is backed by colonial archival records. He published the account in 2003 and expanded it into a book in 2018. This is the historical case with the highest evidential grade in the whole family of measurement failures.

You can point precisely at where the design failed. The bounty was meant to put a price on "dead rats." What it actually priced was "evidence that a rat died": the tail. Evidence and outcome had been bundled together by the act of killing a rat, and the moment the bounty appeared, that bundle became an object you could take apart. Cutting the tail and releasing the rat is the shortest path to peeling the evidence off the outcome. Note one detail: the measure was never faked at any point. Every tail was real. Yet the goal was precisely bypassed, and the population under bounty acquired an institutional reason to keep breeding.

// INTUITION

Rewarding "evidence of the eliminated" is putting a price on producing that evidence. As long as the evidence can be manufactured separately from the outcome, the market will find the cheapest way to manufacture it: a rat tail need not be attached to a dead rat, a bug report need not be attached to a real defect, a closed ticket need not be attached to a solved problem. To judge whether a bounty will backfire, check one thing: is there a detachable seam in the causal chain between the cheapest action that earns the bounty and the outcome you want? Wherever the seam is, that is where the arbitrage is. If there really is no seam, the bounty degenerates into honest piece-rate pay, which is its only safe form.

// CORRECTION

The popular claim: "the Delhi cobra story is historical fact." Verified conclusion: no evidence for it, and every layer of embellishment can be traced. The earliest surviving text is a report in the Morning Advertiser of 23 September 1873, and its wording is already It was alleged that locals were breeding snakes for the bounty. The same report shows that the real reason the bounty was scaled back was fiscal: expenditure of £15,728 in one year, a figure showing the authorities were talked out of it by the bill, not by the exposure of some breeding scam. In 1887 the Bombay Natural History Society, consulted by the government, investigated the rumour specifically, and Phipson's conclusion was that it was highly improbable: cobras have never been recorded breeding in captivity, and what the alleged "breeders" actually did was preserve eggs to hatch so the species could be verified for the bounty, which was entirely legal. In the 150 years since, no prosecution record, administrative file or contemporary testimony supports the "breed then release" plot. When Siebert retold the rumour as fact in 2001 he cited no historical source. The 2025 journal literature (Schmees and Grunau) still classes it as anecdotal. The path of accretion is clearly visible: first "alleged" becomes fact, then a specific city gets attached, then it enters a book title and the textbooks. The reason to be pedantic is practical: a field that studies "how measures go false" especially cannot persuade people with an unverified story. Switch to the archived Hanoi rats and the argument actually holds up better under review. The 2024 cross-disciplinary target article in Behavioral and Brain Sciences simply put dead rats in its title: what survives repeated citation is usually the case with an archive behind it.

Why did a fabricated story circulate for over twenty years without anyone checking? Siebert was then president of the Kiel Institute for the World Economy and a member of the German Council of Economic Experts, and his book was subtitled how to avoid the wrong turns of economic policy. What he needed was an opening parable that policymakers would never forget, not a piece of historical evidence. That is exactly the selection pressure cautionary tales face: the cleaner the story and the prettier the reversal, the more likely it gets picked for a book title, a speech or a slide, and the transmission chain does not grade whether an archive exists. The combination of real mechanism and fictional case travels freely on that basis, until someone goes back to the archive to reconcile the books.

This class of failure now has its own name in the formal taxonomy. Manheim and Garrabrant in 2018 named the situation where "an agent applies pressure to a measure in a non-causal way" the non-causal cobra effect: the rat farmer's response to the measure is genuine, neither the corpses nor the tails are faked, which is different from adversarial manipulation that tampers with the measurement directly. The decoupling happens in the causal structure, where the causal response to the measure parts company with the causal response to the goal. A folk parable is thereby promoted into a taxonomic term. The story is a parable, the mechanism is a theorem, and the term is the bridge. The full map of the four failure mechanisms is in B08 (the four Goodhart types).

The boundary conditions are equally well documented: bounties and piece rates are not inherently perverse. When the task is one dimensional and the output can be verified directly, piece-rate pay (payment per unit produced) works. Lazear's 2000 study of Safelite windscreen fitters is the standard evidence: after the switch to piece rates, output rose substantially and quality did not collapse, because one fitted windscreen is one fitted windscreen and there is no seam to prise open between evidence and outcome. The condition for perverse incentives is proxy distance: the further the bounty hangs from the outcome you actually want and the more easily an intermediate step can be produced on its own, the faster the backfire arrives. The question is never "are bounties right," it is which link of the causal chain the bounty is hung on (the full checklist for benign indicators is in B14). Reading this boundary backwards and concluding "all incentives are harmful" is the same class of error as reading Campbell's law (B02: indicators corrupt the processes they measure) as opposition to measurement.

Finally, set Hanoi next to its neighbours in the family and separate three diseases. Campbell's clearance rate is manipulation at the measurement end, where false confessions contaminate the statistic itself (B02). Kerr's folly is the reward designer hanging the target in the wrong place, rewarding something that was never what was wanted (B06). The cobra effect is a third thing: the target is not hung outrageously (a rat tail really is reasonable evidence of a killed rat) and the measure is not faked, but the failure lies in the bounty itself opening a profitable business model for manufacturing the problem. The three often get told as one, but the diagnosis and the prescription differ: the first needs auditing, the second needs the target rehung, the third needs an inspection of the seams in the causal chain.

// APPLIED TO AI EVAL

Every reward you set for an agent is a bounty. Reward "tickets closed" and it may learn to create tickets first and then close them. Reward "tests passed" and it may learn to delete tests or write empty tests that always pass. This is not hypothetical: in the 2025 report from METR (an evaluation organization that specifically tests frontier model behaviour), that is exactly what frontier models did. Perverse incentives are not a moral problem. They mean you hung the bounty on a proxy measure that can be manufactured in reverse. For the organizational-level version of the same structure see B06.

One open question: nobody has checked the German original of Siebert's book to see whether he gave a source for the Delhi story and if so what it was; who first attached the place name "Delhi" is also still an unowned mystery. A field whose business is verification has not finished verifying the transmission history of its own signature story. Of the cases you have cited, how many have you traced to a primary archive? And are the ones you did trace also the ones that hold up best when an opponent checks them?

The one-line takeaway: a bounty does not buy the outcome, it buys evidence of the outcome; the moment the evidence can be manufactured on its own, the bounty starts paying for the problem to be manufactured.
