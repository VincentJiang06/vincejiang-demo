Discipline & Surveillance

Why organizations cannot buffer themselves from a ranking.

Requires R01 Reactivity Unlocks R09 Audit Society R13 Boycott, Exit & Plurality K02 Governance: Targets & Terror R14 The Emotional Machine of Rankings

Why can nobody shake off the rankings? Organisation theory has a famous old prediction called decoupling: organisations are experts at outward compliance and business as usual inside. The classic papers, Meyer and Rowan in 1977 and DiMaggio and Powell in 1983, both say that for legitimacy an organisation need only perform symbolic conformity, and educational organisations, whose output is hard to observe, are old hands at this. By that prediction, law schools should have easily treated the rankings as decoration, especially since rankings have every feature that ought to invite lip service: new, externally imposed, expensive and hard to manage, openly despised by internal elites. But you already saw the reality in R01: aid cut, lost graduates chased, two sets of books, and not one school treating it as decoration. The sociological answer is that a ranking is a form of disciplinary power, which works not by coercion but by keeping you continuously visible. Whether a ruler can be dodged comes down to a few switches.

The paradox is glaring. What Sauder and Espeland found in 2009 was not successful buffering but the opposite, tight coupling: Yale's dean called the rankings an "idiot poll," Harvard's dean called them "Mickey Mouse," and for all the contempt both still watched their positions closely. So they inverted the question: instead of asking why organisations can decouple, ask why some environmental pressures are more resistant to decoupling than others. Their explanatory tool is borrowed from the French philosopher Foucault: a ranking is disciplinary power, and the paper quotes "discipline is an art of rank." It works not through the whip but by keeping its object continuously visible: capillary, continuous, diffuse, operating through the most mundane daily procedures.

Why did buffering fail? It comes down to four properties of rankings: commensurable, relative, widely circulated, zero-sum. Zero-sum is the decisive one: one school rising necessarily means another falling, so you cannot get away with symbolic compliance, because if others do not comply you drop. That gives an empirical rule: a public, zero-sum, commensurated measure is almost impossible for an organisation to buffer away. All three features of surveillance show up in the flesh in the interviews. One dean compared publication day to "closing your eyes before turning on the light so the cockroaches have time to run"; another admitted "when I make decisions I'm always watching the rankings, not what's best for the school." The distance-surveillance point also borrows Scott's concept: rankings let dispersed lay audiences (applicants, trustees, state legislators) "see into" a law school, achieving governing at a distance. The precondition, that an object must first be made readable before outsiders can see into it, has a whole lesson of its own, R05.

Normalization's five processes are an assembly line: first set all law schools as one kind (comparison), then differentiate them precisely (differentiation), then arrange them into a unique hierarchy (hierarchization). The hierarchy creates an abstract "optimal law school" that everyone moves toward, admissions come to recognise only LSAT and GPA, the student body becomes homogeneous and racial diversity suffers (homogenization), and those who deviate from the template are excluded and stigmatised (exclusion). Low-ranked schools are judged "substandard" even when fully ABA accredited, and their students joke on forums that they come from a "third-tier toilet." Discipline also leaves institutional fossils: Chun and Sauder documented in 2023 how "ranking expertise" grew into a new occupation, with schools creating dedicated posts to manage the data reported to the rankings, and internal dependency relations reorganising around them. An external measure ends up living inside the measured in the form of a headcount line.

Of internalization's three emotional channels, anxiety is the most structural: rankings are relative, unstable and highly visible, and deans have "more responsibility than control" over their position, a situation identified in Marmot's health sociology as one of the most damaging. Interviewees said that even when the rank went up, "you only get to be happy for 15 minutes," and then you start worrying about next year. The paradox of resistance has precedents: Burawoy's shop-floor workers "making out" and Covaleski and colleagues' work on the big accounting firms both show that gaming a control system deepens investment in it. The law school version is the AALS commissioning scholars to attack the rankings' methodology and mailing a signed condemnation to LSAT takers, and every public confrontation turned the rankings further into "the reference point around which beliefs get organised." Gaming works the same way, and the authors reinterpret it as resistance that looks like decoupling but actually deepens internalization: it challenges the ranking's legitimacy as an accurate representation, yet naturalises the ranking further because more is invested in it. The paper's closing formulation is worth keeping whole: rankings both seduce and coerce, and doing so in the flat language of numbers makes them more powerful, not less.

Disciplinary force has one key volume knob: monopoly. Sauder and Espeland compared law schools with business schools in 2006. The former faced a near-monopoly, full-coverage U.S. News ranking; the latter faced at least five lists with different methods and inconsistent results, so schools could pick the flattering one to tell a story about, and discretion and loose coupling came back. The more rankings there are, the weaker any one ranking's disciplinary force. But they also pointed out a paradox: hedging against monopoly by "making more rankings" further establishes rankings as the legitimate form of accountability in higher education, so the means of resistance entrenches the thing resisted. This thread leads to the exit politics of R13 and the institutional defences of G06. The latest reflection from inside the camp lands on the same point: Chun in 2025 argued that early work (including Sauder's own 2008 paper treating U.S. News as an "intruder") focused too much on a single dominant ranking, while in reality measures coexist in plurality, conflict with each other and are continuously contested, so the disciplinary model needs rewriting for that plurality.

The withdrawal wave from 2022 onward (told in R01) provides very clean field evidence for this rule: top law schools led by Yale and Harvard collectively refused to submit data, U.S. News switched to public data and kept ranking them while heavily revising its methodology, and the standings then swung violently. Resistance did not end the ranking, it just bought a ruler with different markings, which fits the 2009 paper's claim that resistance is a constituent part of power. Still, leave room for the other side: Englund and Gerdin in 2020 proposed the opposite of reactive conformance, documenting scholars who under certain conditions (long evaluation cycles, strong peer support, plenty of alternative sources of reputation) really can suppress conformity pressure temporarily; Slager and Gond in 2022 observed ambivalence in the ESG ratings field, where firms accept and resist a rating at the same time. Reactivity is a political process with room for discretion, not a mechanical transmission: internalization is hard to avoid, but "inevitable" overstates it.

// CORRECTION

Popular claim: "Discipline just means Big Brother surveillance." Checked: discipline is productive, it produces skills, subjectivities and the habit of self-scanning; and it is diffuse and mundane, operating through the most ordinary procedures, with no central watcher required. Also, Sauder and Espeland do not just apply Foucault, they criticise him for ignoring the organisational dimension: how discipline gets divided, coordinated and evaluated between organisations. They have words for their own camp too: contemporary institutional theory treats institutions as macro symbolic systems and has lost the early micro orientation, and needs what Hallett and Ventresca call an "inhabited institutions" perspective to bring people back in.

// INTUITION

What defeats buffering is not how tight the surveillance is, it is the shape of the measure. Commensurable, relative, widely circulated, zero-sum, and of the four, zero-sum is the deadliest: it prices "doing nothing" directly as "falling." Ordinary institutional pressure follows a threshold logic, where a compliant posture is enough to discharge it; a zero-sum ranking follows a positional logic, where your place is jointly determined by other people's behaviour, so symbolic compliance does not exist mathematically. To judge whether an organisation can buffer a ruler away, first ask whether it is a threshold system or a positional one.

// APPLIED TO AI EVAL

A public leaderboard makes labs visible to each other. Nobody can shelve it as symbolic compliance: if you do not chase the score and a competitor does, you drop. Every run at the leaderboard, and even every public criticism of it, turns it further into the reference point around which the whole field's beliefs are organised, deepening its disciplinary force. Resistance and conformity arrive at the same destination here. The claim is testable: count citations of papers criticising Chatbot Arena after 2023 and see whether they rise as criticism increases. That count has not been done.

One open question: if top-school withdrawal, public criticism and methodological reform all fail to end a ranking, where is the exit from the disciplinary loop? Would multiple conflicting rankings coexisting dilute disciplinary force, or just let every school pick one more ruler that flatters it?

The one-line takeaway: a public, zero-sum, visible-to-all ranking is power that needs no overseer; cursing it, gaming it and quitting it all grease its wheels.
